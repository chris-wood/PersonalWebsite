Christopher A. Wood

I am a recent graduate from the Rochester Institute of Technology (RIT), where I completed a dual BS degree in Computer Science and Software Engineering with an accelerated BS/MS option in Computer Science. I also have minors and concentrations in Mathematics and Writing Studies. In the fall I will start my PhD at the University of California Irvine, working under Dr. Gene Tsudik. In the mean time I am an intern at Xerox PARC working as a security and privacy researcher in the Computer Science Laboratory.

My research interests are very diverse, and include everything from applied cryptography, computational graph theory, computer and network security, and combinatorics. Fortunately, I have had many opportunities at RIT and beyond to pursue research projects in these areas, which have resulted in several publications, presentations, and unpublished technical reports. As a student I try to be involved in projects that span several disciplines to exercise my skills in multiple fields. I also actively pursue teaching assistant positions to share my passion for science and technology with younger students.

In my spare time I also enjoy actively working on my own side projects that are typically unrelated to my main areas of research. These are usually an excuse to learn a new programming language, library, framework, or other technology. The remainder of whatever free time I have left is devoted to music and exercise. My main musical interests are hard rock and heavy metal (i.e. bands such as Avenged Sevenfold, In Flames, Lamb of God, August Burns Red, and Trivium), but I also branch out into acoustic rock, jazz, and classical music. I am the proud owner of a Schecter Hellraiser C-1 (a black model), and am slowly teaching myself the ropes.

Computational Techniques for Narrowing Edge Folkman Number BoundsRIT

Combinatorics, Computational Graph TheoryJanuary 2013 - present

Advisor: Dr. Stanislaw Radziszowski (CS)

I am investigating various computational techniques to prove (or disprove) the conjecture that the edge Folkman number F(3,3;4) is at most 127. Our main work was done by reducing the question of G -> (3,3;4) to an equivalent 3-SAT formula, and then modifying the structure of this formula to aid SAT solvers in the decision process. We also applied this SAT-based decision technique to other interesting Folkman problems, and are investigating the relative effectiveness of using SAT vs SDP solvers for Folkman numbers. My progress on this work is maintained here.

Secure Logging System for Cloud-Based ApplicationsRIT

Cryptography, Computer Security, Secure Software DesignJuly 2012 - May 2013

Advisors: Dr. Rajendra K. Raj (CS) and Dr. Andy Meneely (SE)

I am developing a secure logging architecture for cloud-based SaaS applications that utilizes ciphertext-policy attribute based encryption (CP-ABE) for log file confidentiality and authenticated hash chains for log file integrity. It features offline public and online private verifiability of the log files to detect modification, addition, deletion, and truncation attacks.

Wireless Ad-hoc Network Group Key Management RIT

Applied Cryptography, Wireless NetworkingMay 2011 - present

Colleagues: Dr. Marcin Lukowiak (CE), Dr. Stanislaw Radziszowski (CS), Dr. Peter Bajorski (Statistics), Professor Alan Kaminsky (CS), and Dr. Michael Kurdziel (Harris RF)

We are investigating group key management protocols for wireless ad-hoc radio networks with constrained channel bandwidths and computational power. Our current solution is a protocol based on the standardized Internet Key Exchange protocol. My role in this team is to implement network simulators in SMURPH/SIDE and NS-3 to obtain empirical performance measurements, and also conduct experiments with Monte Carlo simulations to verify the correctness of our protocol statistical model. We have a patent pending on our protocol.


In the second refresh of this project, we focused on extending our statistical model to support unbounded child nodes and arbitrary message lengths in our tree-based key distribution model. I was responsible for implementing the statistical model and corresponding simulation in Matlab for verification purposes.

L(2,1)-Labeling ProblemRIT

Computational Graph TheorySeptember 2011 - present

Advisor: Dr. Jobby Jacob (Mathematics)

Phase 2 (December 2012 - present)

In the current refresh of this project we are studying the L(2,1) span of bicubic graphs, which are 3-regular bipartite graphs.

Phase 1 (September 2011 - November 2012)

I worked on defining a characterization of the L(2,1)-span of trees based on their structural properties. I implemented a labeling algorithm based on dynamic programming to assist in the study of tree characterization, which helped to develop tree construction algorithms that can be used to produce infinitely many trees with a specific L(2,1)-span. Building upon this result, we then found a complete L(2,1) label span characterization of all trees with up to twenty vertices.

Rootkit Design, Implementation, and Detection in Secure Operating SystemsRIT

Computer Security, Operating Systems, Malware DesignMay 2009 - August 2009, March 2011 - June 2011

Advisor: Dr. Rajendra K. Raj (CS)

I examined rootkit malware that targeted the Windows NT family of operating systems. This study included user-mode and kernel-mode rootkit implementations and state-of-the-art static and dynamic techniques. I developed a kernel filter driver for the keyboard device driver stack in C to help determine the presence of a specific kind of keystroke-monitoring malware. In the latter half of this project, I researched secure operating system design principles at all levels of the software stack. The main deliverable was a case study for popular operating systems built for various purposes, including Microsoft Singularity, Chrome OS, Android, QNX, and Microsoft Azure. Arithmetics August 2013 - present

Arithmetics is a simple iOS app that emulates the tile word matching puzzles that used to plague video games back in the day (think Mario Party on N64), except that algebraic expressions, rather than words, are behind each tile. In order to find a pair of matching tiles the player must remember and pick the two tiles that evaluate to the same result. Too much to remember? Probably. I really wanted to start dabbling with iOS though, and this was the first thing that came to mind.


Galois Field Arithmetic Library March 2013 - present

I am implementing a library for Galois field arithmetic in both polynomial and normal basis representations for proper composite fields of any number of extensions. The library is meant to be used for educational purposes with small fields, so performance is not a primary goal. Future work will include implementing elliptic curve arithmetic (e.g. point addition, multiplication, etc) over these fields.


Circuit Minimization Library March 2013 - present

I am implementing a variety of small programs to optimize nonlinear and linear (XOR gates only) circuits with varying goals. Most of the techniques are based on algorithms and heuristics found in the literature, while some are my own creation. The goal is to optimize circuits of interest in cryptography, such as the linear circuits used to perform basis changes between finite field representations, or nonlinear circuits to compute the multiplicative inverse over an arbitrary finite field. Circuit minimization is a difficult problem, so some of my solutions rely on parallel programming to tackle ``large'' instances, and since this whole library is written in Java, I am leverage the Parallel Java library developed by Professor Alan Kaminsky.


AskSG - A Community Overlay for the RIT Student Government December 2012 - May 2013

AskSG is a comprehensive platform that corrals student ideas, questions, and concerns from a variety of online sources into a single portal for the RIT Student Government (SG). The goal is to enhance their relationship with students and to provide a channel for advice on what the governance body should focus on, such as programs or policies on campus to change, replace or recreate. AskSG provides a common interface for all directed student questions and inquiries to provide an overarching picture of student sentiment. This interface, which meets federal accessibility requirements, will funnel student questions submitted through SMS, phone calls, and social media private messaging, and then enable responses to be reciprocated using the same incoming interface.


Parallel 3-SAT February 2013 - May 2013

The goal of this project is to implement a 3-SAT solver using a parallel computing framework, namely, the Parallel Java library developed by Professor Alan Kaminsky.


As part of a three person team with Eitan Romanoff and Ankur Bajoria, we developed a parallel and sequential version of a brute force 3-SAT satisfiability checking algorithm, documented the performance gains, and presented our work. While nothing novel, it was a fun and interesting application of parallel computing to one of Karp's famous NP-complete problems.


ABLS - Attribute Based Logging System July 2012 - present (inactive development)

ABLS is a logging service for cloud applications that provides atrribute-based log confidentiality and high data-integrity guarantees. It is intended to aid application developers in storing system events that can be used to build audit trails. Such audit trails can be used to enforce organizational security policies and support user-based non-repudiation through manual and automated audits.


Wedge - A Cross-Platform Password Cracking Utility November 2012 - February 2013

Wedge is a cross-platform password cracking utility that mimics the behavior of John the Ripper. It currently supports standard dictionary and brute-force attacks for a variety of unsalted password digests, but I expect to integrate more advanced password cracking techniques, such as the use of rainbow tables and those based on Markov-chains, in the future.


Continuous Authentication System September 2012 - November 2012

This project is a prototype service for *nix-based operating systems that captures user typing patterns in real-time and uses this information to identify the user. It is the result of my research on continuous authentication systems and keyloggers. The system features a C-based keylogger that streams data to a classification and authentication engine written in Python. Users must enroll in the system via scripted typing sessions in order to be identified.
